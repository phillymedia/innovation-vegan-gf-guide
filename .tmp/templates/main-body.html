    Dishes to trySeitan cheesesteak, haymaker pizza, their most popular pizza, complete with Violife cheese, oregano, red onions, and seitan sausage; and habanero buffalo wings
    The lowdown

    Blackbird Pizzeria, an all-vegan BYOB bar food joint, is a well-recognized staple for Philadelphia vegans -- its seitan cheesesteak earned it the title of Winner of the Best Vegan Cheesesteak in Philly contest in both 2016 and 2017, and it also made the top 10 vegan wings in the U.S. in 2014. Rather than using the ubiquitous Daiya cheese, a commercially available vegan cheese made of coconut oil and tapioca, owner Mark Mebus uses Violife cheese, as the Inquirer reported in 2018. According to Mebus, Violife is ‘the most meltable and convincing plant-based cheese he has yet discovered.’ He would know - he was part of the opening team for a past endeavor, Horizons, by Philly vegan scene pioneers, Rich Landau and Kate Jacoby.


    A tipBlackbird does not carry gluten-free bread or crusts, so the only options for gluten-free diners there are the arugula and green salads.

    The lowdown

    Bareburger, an international burger chain whose Philly location has table service and a beer and wine menu, has one of the largest menus you’ll ever see - they claim it’s possible to create 81 million different burgers. Though they offer a large array of meat options as well -- bison and buttermilk-fried chicken, for example-- vegans rave about its separate vegan menu with options like vegan gouda, plant-based eggs, Beyond bratwurst, and five different types of vegan burgers (including both Impossible burgers and Beyond Meat burgers). Two of their most popular vegan dishes are the Duchess, an Impossible burger with garlic aioli, mushrooms, caramelized onions, baby kale and --get this-- smoked gouda, which mirrors the Duke burger on their non-vegan menu, and the crispy brussels sprouts (vegan and gluten-free; on the heavier side, as they are fried). Their emphasis on organic ingredients makes it a bit on the pricier end of the spectrum for burger joints, but most if not all of their food, including vegan options, is hearty and rich, and they have everything vegans usually miss out on, including coconut-and-hemp milk milkshakes.


    A tipThe menu is marked up to show which items are vegan and gluten-free, as well as which have nuts and GMOs. Their menu also clearly marks their celiac-safe gluten-free options, which include buns and a collard green wrap.

    Dishes to tryBanana funnel cake (vegan and gluten-free); vegan corn dog with royale sauce (a sauce similar to Thousand Island dressing); ‘fancy’ sweet potato battered corndog with chorizo (gluten-free).
    The lowdown

    Fox & Sons Fancy Corndogs, a carnival-esque food stand that opened at Reading Terminal in Feb. 2017, struck a happy medium when they found a batter for their fried foods that happened to be gluten-free. The owners were inspired by the texture of Japanese and Chinese pastries, co-owner Rebecca Foxman said, which typically use a blend of rice flour and corn starch. Their entirely gluten-free menu includes corn dogs, fried broccoli bites, fried cheese curds, funnel cake, and poutine (including a vegan option). The vegan iteration of their funnel cake, the owners report, also saves food waste: they use ripe bananas that produce stands at Reading Terminal would get rid of at the end of the day to replace the buttermilk and eggs in their traditional funnel cake recipe. The store also recently appeared on the Rachael Ray Show.


    A tipFor those who eat meat but not dairy, you can order a regular corndog with vegan batter. They have many dairy-free extras like Daiya cheese that you can use to customize your order.

    You won’t see ‘tempeh sausage’ or ‘soy-based chicken’ on the menu at V Street, a plant-based, vegan restaurant and bar. James Beard-nominated chefs Rich Landau and Kate Jacoby opened V Street in 2014 as a lower-cost (but still pricey) offshoot of the wildly successful Vedge. They took inspiration from street foods around the world to create a menu that touches India (Cauliflower ‘65,’ made of crunchy papadum crackers and fried cauliflower florets in a spicy sauce), Peru (fried potato wedges with a spicy aioli), Jamaica (jerk tofu over creamy sweet potato aioli), Ethiopia and Eritrea (berbere broccoli), and Iran (Falooda, their take on the traditional Persian rosewater ice cream dish). In his glowing three-bell review of V Street, Inquirer food critic Craig LaBan writes, ‘The way Rich Landau sees it, with animal products off his vegan table, he can't cook with half the ingredients that other chefs do: ‘So why limit ourselves to one ethnicity?’”


    A tipV Street offers soy-free and gluten-free menus to guests with dietary restrictions. They also can modify their menu to accommodate other allergies if you call in advance.

    Go forA slightly-fancier version of the college pizza + beer night out (replace beer with wine, pepperoni with prosciutto, and pop with blues).

    Dishes to tryThe Diavolo pizza on a gluten-free crust; the spaghetti squash; and the gluten-free cheesecake.
    The lowdown

    Zavino, while known for its pizza, is not a pizza joint. The white marble tables, fully-stocked bar and antipasti station in the restaurant tell you immediately that though they serve reasonably priced pies, they also cater to those who seek  to try their $23 tasting board. Though they have very few vegan options, their spaghetti squash small plate, which might sound like something from a deprivation diet, has an incredibly hearty flavor from the sage, garlic, roasted pine nuts in its tomato sauce. “Surely these pies are better than 98 percent of the competition,” Craig LaBan wrote in 2010, but “the pizzas aren't even nearly the best things to eat at Zavino.” The pizza is a must-try for gluten-intolerant diners, however: at the University City location, you can watch a chef spin their made-on-site gluten-free crust behind the bar (fluffy with a crunch from the wood-fired oven, it’s known in the gluten-free community as the best crust in the city).


    A tipThey don’t carry vegan cheese, but their pizzas can be made vegan and/or dairy free. They can make other items dairy-free, such as the brussels sprouts, spaghetti squash, the roasted cauliflower, the almond cake, and all of their salads. Their gluten-free crusts, however, contain milk.
